Summary

  • Investigating the interaction between environmental heterogeneity and local adaptation is critical to understand the evolutionary history of a species, providing the premise for studying the response of organisms to rapid climate change. However, for most species how exactly the spatial heterogeneity promotes population divergence and how genomic variations contribute to adaptive evolution remain poorly understood.

  • We examine the contributions of geographical and environmental variables to population divergence of the relictual, alpine herb Circaeaster agrestis as well as genetic basis of local adaptation using RAD-seq and plastome data.

  • We detected significant genetic structure with an extraordinary disequilibrium of genetic diversity among regions, and signals of isolation-by-distance along with isolation-by-resistance. The populations were estimated to begin diverging in the late Miocene, along with a possible ancestral distribution of the Hengduan Mountains and adjacent regions. Both environmental gradient and redundancy analyses revealed significant association between genetic variation and temperature variables. Genome-environment association analyses identified 16 putatively adaptive loci related to biotic and abiotic stress resistance.

  • Our genome wide data provide new insights into the important role of environmental heterogeneity in shaping genetic structure, and access the footprints of local adaptation in an ancient relictual species.

Introduction

A primary goal of phylogeography is understanding how geographical and ecological factors shape the genetic structure and evolutionary history of species. Environmental factors can act in concert and vary in space, resulting in divergent natural selection leading to genetic divergence during adaptation to heterogeneous environments (Savolainen et al., 2007). Such genetic divergence along environmental gradients, or across varied ecological habitats, can be indicative of local adaptation, a mechanism that is beneficial to the long persistence of a population and intensify differentiation among populations (Kawecki & Ebert, 2004; Conover et al., 2009; Colautti & Barrett, 2013; Savolainen et al., 2013; Lowry et al., 2019). Understanding the genetic basis of local adaptation will provide deeper insights into the response of organisms to ongoing climate change, aiding efforts to the conservation of genetic resources (Aitken et al., 2008; Ahrens et al., 2019).

On the one hand, divergent natural selection is the driving force of genetic divergence (Darwin, 1859; Coyne & Orr, 2004), while on the other hand, gene flow can buffer genetic differentiation (Spieth, 1974; Goicoechea et al., 2019). Evolution resulting from local adaptation depends on both the strength of local selective pressures and the homogenizing effect of connectivity among different localities. Additionally, genetic drift may reinforce the establishment and maintenance of genetic divergence, particularly in populations with small effective size (Wright, 1946; Kimura & Crow, 1964). Therefore, quantifying the relative contributions among natural selection, genetic drift and population connectivity to genetic divergence is crucial for understanding the role of local adaptation in adaptive evolution of a species (Savolainen et al., 2007; Friis et al., 2018).

Phenotypic patterns that are associated with divergent selection have been observed in model plants (Mitchell-Olds et al., 2007; Anderson et al., 2011; Leinonen et al., 2013; Zou et al., 2017). However, identification of genetic loci that govern phenotypic variations in response to environmental pressures is a challenging task, especially for non-model species with limited genomic resources. Facilitated by advancements in sequencing technology and population genomics, it is now possible to study hundreds of thousands of loci improving our understanding of genome-wide effects of accumulating genetic divergence and genomic properties that influence the process of local adaptation (McCormack et al., 2013; Ellegren, 2014; Seehausen et al., 2014; Weigel & Nordborg, 2015). In particular, highly divergent loci identified by Genome-Environment Association (GEA) analyses can be interpreted as potential targets of divergent selection associated with population-specific environmental covariables (Coop et al., 2010; Rellstab et al., 2015; Hoban et al., 2016; Forester et al., 2018). In this context, plant studies are poised to reveal the genomic signatures underlying the evolution of local adaptation (e.g. Seehausen et al., 2014; Ahrens et al., 2019). However, for most species, such as relictual alpine herbs, how exactly the spatial heterogeneity promotes population diversification and how genomic variations contribute to adaptive evolution, in particular their interaction, remains poorly understood.

In the present study, we focus on investigating the evolutionary history and local adaptation of Circaeaster agrestis Maxim, an annual alpine herb with a relatively narrow habitat (Fu & Bartholomew, 2001; Sun, Y et al., 2017). Circaeaster Maxim., with its only alliance Kingdonia Balf.f. & W.W. Smith, constitute the early-diverging eudicot family Circaeasteraceae (Ranunculales) (The Angiosperm Phylogeny et al., 2016). Due to special morphological characters such as open dichotomous leaf venation, this genus is of great interest to many botanists (Foster, 1971; Ren et al., 2003). Circaeaster exhibits low morphological diversity with only one species, garnering a status of critical endangerment (Wild Plants Under State Protection in China). The distributions of C. agrestis is confined to the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau (QTP) and adjacent areas where exhibit a wide range of elevations from 2,100-5,000 m (Fu & Bartholomew, 2001). The uplift of QTP has created extraordinarily geomorphological and climatic diversity in this area. Due to the high mountain barrier formed by the Himalayas in the south, the central and western QTP (i.e. the Himalayas) are characterized by a cold and dry climate (Favre et al., 2015). In contrast, the eastern QTP (i.e. the Hengduan Mountains) and adjacent arears are associated with deep valleys and characterized mainly by a warm and wet climate (Ren et al., 2017). Given the high degree of geographical and ecological heterogeneity, QTP comprises a promising model system for studying genetic signatures of local adaptation.

Specifically, we generated and analyzed two types of genomic datasets: single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) derived from the restriction site associated DNA sequencing (RAD-Seq) of 18 C. agrestis populations and 20 plastome sequences of C. agrestis populations with other Ranunculales representatives. We aim to illustrate the evolutionary patterns of population divergence interacting with environmental variables and access the potential genetic basis of local adaptation. Here, we raise the following questions: 1) how alpine environments affect genetic structure within species and drive population divergence, and 2) how genetic variation is associated with local adaptation? Answering these questions will help us understand species adaptation to rapidly changing climate, and aid in conservation of small patchy diversity in biodiversity hotspots.

Materials and Methods

Sampling, RAD library preparation and sequencing

A total of 139 individuals of C. agrestis from 18 localities were sampled (Table S1). Sampling locations were chosen based on existing occurrence records covering the geographic and climatic distribution of the species (Fig. 1). All materials were dried and stored in silica gel, and all the voucher specimens were deposited in Herbarium of Wuhan Botanical Garden (HIB) (Table S1). Total DNA was extracted from silica gel-dried leaves with a modified CTAB (Cetyl trimethylammonium bromide) method (Yang et al., 2014). For RAD library construction and sequencing, genomic DNA was digested with the restriction enzyme EcoRI in a 30 ul reaction. T4 ligase was used to ligate a P1 adapter to the fragments. The adapter-ligated fragments were subsequently pooled, randomly sheared, and size-selected with a length of 350–550 bp. DNA was then ligated to a second adapter (P2), a Y adapter hat has divergent ends to the chosen DNA fragments. The ligation products were purified and PCR amplified, followed by gel purification, and finally size selected to 350-550 bp. Agilent 2100 Bioanaylzer and qPCR were used to qualify and quantify the sample library. Paired-end sequencing was performed on two lanes of Illumina HiSeq 2000 platform at BGI-Shenzhen (Shenzhen, Guangdong, China).

For plastome sequencing, 20 samples covering all 18 localities were chosen. Then the libraries were quantified using an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA) and real-time quantitative PCR. Libraries were multiplexed and sequenced using a 2×125 bp run on one lane of Illumina HiSeq 2000 platform at BGI-Shenzhen (Shenzhen, Guangdong, China).

Processing of Illumina data

For RAD-sequencing, pair-end Illumina reads were processed into RAD-tags using the STACKS v.2.30 software pipeline (Catchen et al., 2013). Samples were initially demultiplexed with PROCESS_RADTAGS. Reads with an average Phred score of at least 30, an unambiguous barcode, and restriction cut site were retained. Then the perl wrapper DENOVO_MAP.PL was used to identify optimal parameters by executing USTACKS, CSTACKS, and SSTACKS (Catchen et al., 2013). Following the suggested protocol, we investigated a range of parameter values with M and n values ranging from 1 to 9 (fixing M = n) and m = 3 (Rochette & Catchen, 2017). We plotted two statistics that were useful in making the appropriate parameter choice: the number of polymorphic loci shared across most samples (the r80 loci) and the distribution of the number of SNPs per locus.

We filtered the catalog of reads using the POPULATIONS module in the STACKS pipeline to produce datasets for downstream population genetic analyses. Polymorphic RAD loci that presented in all 139 individuals were retained for subsequent analyses. To validate the influence of missing data, we also employed a loose parameter of - r = 0.8, in which at least 80% of individuals in a population were required to process a locus for that population. Potential homologs were excluded by removing loci showing heterozygosity > 0.5 within samples. We further filtered our dataset with a minor allele frequency (MAF) > 0.01 and kept only biallelic SNPs. Finally, per locus, we filtered our dataset by selecting the most informative SNP based on the number of minor alleles.

For genome-skimming sequencing, raw sequence reads were subsequently filtered using Trimmomatic v.0.36 (Bolger et al., 2014) by removing duplicate reads and adapter-contaminated reads. Remaining high-quality reads were directly mapped to the reported plastome of C. agrestis (NCBI accession number: KY908400) using Geneious v9.0.2 (Kearse et al., 2012). Initial annotations were implemented in the Plastid Genome Annotator (PGA) (Qu et al., 2019), and then were refined by manual corrections in Geneious. The annotated plastomes were deposited in GenBank under accession numbers xxx-xxx.

Genetic diversity and population structure

We estimated genetic diversity indices including nucleotide diversity (π), expected heterozygosity (He), observed heterozygosity (Ho) and inbreeding coefficients (FIS) using POPULATIONS based on the RAD-seq data. The polymorphic sites in Phylip format were exported from POPULATIONS and were used for calculating haplotypes in DnaSP v6.12 (Rozas et al., 2017). The hierfstat package (Goudet, 2005) in R v3.6.1 (R Team, 2014) was used to estimate population and group level FST using Weir and Cockerham’s method (Weir & Cockerham, 1984).

Population genetic structure was estimated using two methods, Bayesian clustering and principal coordinate analysis (PCoA). Bayesian clustering was performed in STRUCTURE v2.3.4 (Pritchard et al., 2000) with the admixture model. As STRUCTURE analysis assumes that loci are unlinked, we thus filtered out linked SNPs using the –write_single_snp option in the POPULATIONS module (Catchen et al., 2013). To determine the optimal number of groups (K), we ran STRUCTURE 10 times for each K value from 1 through 10. Each run was performed for 200,000 Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) generations with a burn-in period of 100,000 generations. The optimal K was chosen using both delta-K method implemented in STRUCTURE HARVESTER (Earl & vonHoldt, 2012) and cross-entropy criterion implemented in R package LEA v2.8.0 (snmf function) (https://bioconductor.org/packages/LEA/). The coefficient for cluster membership of each individual was averaged across the 10 independent runs using CLUMPP (Jakobsson & Rosenberg, 2007) and plotted using DISTRUCT (Rosenberg, 2004). We performed PCoA analyses using the dartR v1.1.11 package (gl.pcoa function) in R. We tested the overall population structure using the G-statistic test (Goudet et al., 1996) by gstat.randtest function in adegenet v2.1.1 package (Jombart, 2008). Additionally, an analysis of molecular variance (AMOVA) was performed to quantify genetic differentiation among and within populations and genetic groups (as identified by STRUCTURE and PCoA) using Arlequin v3.5 (Excoffier et al., 2007) with significance tests of variance components based on 10,000 permutations.

Population divergence history

We adopted a two-step strategy for investigating the divergent time of C. agrestis. First, we used plastome sequences assembled from Ranunculales and newly sampled C. agrestis populations to estimate the diversification of C. agrestis. A total of 47 plastomes were included (Table S1). The 79 protein coding regions (CDS) were extracted and aligned using MAFFT v7.313 (Katoh & Standley, 2013) and were then concatenated using PhyloSuite v1.1.15 (Zhang et al., 2019). The program BEAST2 v2.5.2 (Bouckaert et al., 2014) was employed for molecular dating with concatenated matrix using a GTR+G+I substitution model selected by jModelTest v2.0.1 (Darriba et al., 2012) under the Bayesian information criterion (BIC). A log-normal relaxed-clock mode and birth-death process were applied. According to fossil records, the time to most recent common ancestors (TMRCA) of Ranunculales and Berberidaceae were constrained to the minimum age of 112 Ma and 72 Ma, respectively. We set both calibrations as an exponential distribution (Ho & Phillips, 2009) with a mean of 1 and an offset (hard bound constraint) that equaled the minimum age of the calibrations. The Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) run was set as 100 million generations with sampling every 1,000 generations. Tracer v1.7.1 (Rambaut et al., 2018) was used to assess the effective sample size (ESS > 200) of each parameter. A maximum clade credibility tree was built by TreeAnnotator v1.8.0 (Rambaut & Drummond, 2010) with the initial 20% of trees discarded as burn-in.

Second, we estimated divergent time between haplotypes of C. agrestis using the SNP sequences generated from RAD-seq. All the parameters of BEAST2 were kept the same as above, with the exception of the TMRCA of C. agrestis populations was constrained based on the previous results. To infer the biogeographic history, ancestral distributions of C. agrestis were estimated by statistical dispersal-extinction-cladogenesis (S-DEC) (Ree & Smith, 2008), as implemented in Reconstruct Ancestral State in Phylogenies (RASP) v4.0 (Yu et al., 2015), using 50,000 randomly sampled trees from posterior distribution generated by BEAST2. We defined the following five areas according to geographic distributions of C. agrestis: A, Qinling Mountain and adjacent regions; B, Northwestern Sichuan-South of Gansu; C, North of Qinghai; D, Hengduan Mountains; E, Himalayas. To detect potential demographic expansions, temporal changes in the effective population size (Ne) were inferred with Extended Bayesian skyline plots (EBSP) as implemented in BEAST2 (Heled & Drummond, 2008). We used the SNP sequences of 139 individuals and the default substitution model. The MCMC was set to 50 million generations with sampling every 5000 generations, and the first 20% was discarded as burn-in. Final graphs were produced with the custom R script provided by Heled (2010), with a burn-in cutoff of 20%.

Effects of heterogeneous landscapes on genetic structure

To illustrate the effects of the heterogeneous landscapes on shaping genetic structure, we conducted Mantel tests for the presence of isolation-by-distance (IBD) and isolation-by-resistance (IBR). To avoid the potential correlation between geographical distance and resistance distance, a partial Mantel test was also conducted to test IBD and IBR by controlling the resistance and geographical distance, respectively. GenAlEx v6.5 (Peakall & Smouse, 2012) was employed to compute the pairwise geographic distance among 18 populations. Resistance distance was generated in CIRCUITSCAPE v4.0.5 based on circuit theory (McRae & Beier, 2007). Circuit resistance provide models of connectivity or resistance to predict patterns of dispersal among sites in a heterogeneous landscape (Dickson et al., 2019). The current ecological niche model (ENM) of environmental niche was calculated in MAXENT (Phillips & Dudik, 2008). We used 19 bioclimatic variables available from the WorldClim2 database (Fick & Hijmans, 2017) at a 30 arc□seconds resolution (Table S1), and actual evapotranspiration (AET; http://www.physicalgeography.net/fundamentals/8j.html) as environmental predictors. To avoid multicollinearity, we ran a Pearson correlation analysis to eliminate one of the variables in each pair with a correlation value higher than 0.9 paired. A total 12 climatic layers was retained for analyses (Table S1).

We used environmental niches representing connectivity between populations as conductance grids to produce pairwise resistance distance, as high habitat suitability was assumed to have low resistance (Nowakowski et al., 2015). Tests of the significance for the relationship between geographical/resistance distances and genetic distance among populations were implemented in the R package ade4 v1.7 (https://CRAN.R-project.org/package=ade4) using mantel.rtest function with 9,999 permutations.

Environmental variables associated with genetic structure

To estimate the contributions of environmental variables to genetic differentiation and to understand the turnover of allele frequencies along an environmental gradient, a gradient forest (GF) analysis was performed using the R package gradientForest v0.1(http://gradientforest.r-forge.r-project.org/). GF is a nonparametric, machine□learning regression tree approach that allows for exploration of nonlinear associations of spatial, environmental, and allelic variables (Gugger et al., 2017; Bay et al., 2018). The analysis partitions the allele frequency data at split values along the environmental gradients and defines the amount of variation explained as ‘split importance’ values (Jiang et al., 2019). The overall predictor importance plot of GF shows the mean importance weighted by allele R2, and the cumulative plot for each predictor shows cumulative change along the environmental gradient. The split importance values are added cumulatively to produce a step-like curve, thus, areas with large steps in a row indicate significant influence on allelic change. The 12 climatic layers used in above analysis was employed as environmental variables in the GF analysis.

A redundancy analysis (RDA) was performed to understand the associations between genetic structure and environmental variables. We estimated the proportion of genetic variance in the populations that is explained by environmental variables using six important variables identified by GF (bio03: isothermality; AET: actual evapotranspiration; bio04: temperature seasonality; bio07: temperature annual range; bio12: annual precipitation and bio15: seasonality precipitation, see results). In our analysis, we constrained the dependent variables (individuals) by the explanatory variables (climate). The RDA analysis was performed using the rda function in the vegan v2.5 package (Oksanen et al., 2018) in R (http://CRAN.R-project.org/package=vegan). The anova.cca function was used to calculate overall significance of the RDA plot, and if this was found to be significant, then significance of each climate variable was calculated, using a significance tests with 9,999 permutations.

Genome□Environment Association (GEA) analysis

To access the evidence of divergent selection acting on the genome and the genetic signatures of local adaptation, tests of associated outlier loci with environmental variation were carried out using BAYESCENV v1.1 (de Villemereuil & Gaggiotti, 2015), which extends the capabilities of the BAYESCAN algorithm by including a model that incorporates environmental data. BAYESCENV has been shown to be fairly robust to isolation by distance and a hierarchically structured scenario (de Villemereuil & Gaggiotti, 2015). For a comprehensive consideration of the environmental effect, the environmental covariable layer was calculated with principal component analysis (PCA) of the six important bioclimatic variables, and the first PC axe (PC1), which explained most of the variability (48.62%), was extracted as the input for BAYESCENV. We ran 20 pilot runs of 5,000 iterations and a burn□in of 50,000 iterations, ultimately obtaining 5,000 MCMC iterations for the analysis. Diagnostics of the log likelihoods and FST values for the 5,000 sampled iterations were checked using the R package coda v0.19 (Plummer et al., 2006) to confirm convergence and sample sizes of at least 2,500.

For functional annotation, the locus consensus sequences of all 6,120 SNPs that were initially generated by POPULATIONS were exported in fasta format. Candidate loci identified in GEA were annotated with Blast2Go v2 (Gotz et al., 2008), which performs automated annotation augmentation by searching the non-redundant Arabidopsis protein database with records from the NCBI ESTs databases (Blastx). All the parameters were set as default with e-value < 1.0E-5.

Results

Sequence data quality and processing

We sequenced 139 individuals of C. agrestis using Illumina that produced a total of 133 gigabyte (Gb) data containing 845,396,236 raw reads (Table S2). After filtering out low quality reads, the average number of used reads per sample was 4,542,944 (median: 4,883,567; Table S2). The depths of coverage for processed samples were ranged from 5.77x (WLG-2) to 22.44x (HZ2-3), with a mean coverage of 11.99x (Table S2). We optimized the parameters for STACKS analysis using five representative samples and identified optimal parameters for our dataset to be M = n =3 (Fig. S1 and S2). There were 3,640,060 loci containing 2,519,268 variant sites after all samples de novo assembling. After strict filtering (- r = 1), we obtained 6,959 RAD loci containing 6,120 SNPs that were used for population genetic analyses. The mean genotyped sites per locus was 146.59bp (stderr 0.03). After a looser filtering (- r = 0.8), we obtained 24,247 RAD loci containing 22,915 variant sites. The sizes of 20 sequenced plastomes from 18 populations of C. agrestis were ranged from 150,979 bp (ZF) to 151,056 bp (SNJ) possessing the same 79 protein coding genes arranged in the same order (Table S3).

Genetic diversity and structure

We detected an extraordinary difference of genetic diversity at population level, especially for the nucleotide diversity π, which ranged from 0.0008 (HZ2) to 0.1646 (DDL). The expected heterozygosity (He) and observed heterozygosity (Ho) were 0.0007 (HZ2) - 0.1555 (DDL) and 0.0014 (HZ2) - 0.2650 (DDL; Table 1), respectively. When a loose filtering strategy was employed, we obtained a similar genetic diversity pattern (Table S4). In total, 66 haplotypes from 139 individuals (Fig. 1; Table S5) were detected, and the haplotype diversity (Hd) was 0.9798. Genetic differentiation between YL and HZ3 is the highest (FST=0.9913), and that between ZG and DDL is the lowest (FST=0.0640; Table S6; Fig. S3a).

Population divergence history

The 47-taxa 79-CDS matrix of Ranunculales used for estimating the original divergence of C. agrestis was 66,117 bp in length containing 12,998 parsimony-informative sites. The divergence of C. agrestis from its closely related Kingdonia was estimated to occur in the early Eocene (stem age, ca. 52.19 Ma; 95% highest posterior density [HPD] intervals, 26.12-83.13 Ma), whereas the early divergence within C. agrestis started in the late□Miocene (ca. 6.33 Ma; 95% HPD, 1.96-23.35 Ma) (Fig. S5). The tree topology inferred by BEAST2 using the SNP sequences generated from RAD-seq identified two main clades (East and West clades) and six sub-groups (E1, E2, W1, W2, W3 and W4 groups), which was consistent with STRUCTURE and PoCA results. The divergence of two main clades was estimated at appropriately 6.71 Ma (95% HPD, 5.18-7.89 Ma). The subsequent divergences between six groups occurred mainly during the Pliocene to late Pleistocene (ca. 5.05Ma-2.90Ma) (Fig. 3a). Biogeographic analysis supported Hengduan Mountains and adjacent regions as the most likely ancestral region for C. agrestise (Fig. 3a and 3b). For the demographic inference, EBSP analysis showed no sign of population expansion or contraction in demographic history of C. agrestis (Fig. 3c).

Effects of topography and ecology

Given that the distribution range of C. agrestis is topographically complex, we evaluated the influence of the heterogeneous landscape in shaping genetic structure via Mantel test. Our analyses suggested both significant patterns of IBD (R2= 0.443, P = 0.001**) and IBR (R2= 0.315, P = 0.017*) (Fig. 4a and b), which were also supported by a partial Mantel test ( ; ). In our case, geographical distance explained the genetic structure better than resistance distances based on current distribution of habitats. We further plotted the conductance grid derived from the IBR model to illustrate the connectivity among populations and thus represent the empirical costs of gene flow through a complex landscape. In general, connectivity gaps among populations were observed in the western clade, reflecting increased habitat isolation in this area resulted from complex topography. In contrast, locations of eastern clade had low resistance to dispersal thus high connectivity among populations (Fig. 4c).

Genome□Environment Association (GEA) analysis

The BAYESCENV result revealed a total of 61 loci have a significant association with environmental variables. We then aligned consensus sequences of these candidate loci for annotation. Discussion

Effects of topography on genetic structure

Our study presents the genetic structure of the palaeoendemic alpine C. agrestis. High haplotype diversity, uneven distribution of genetic diversity, as well as significant IBD and IBR patterns suggest restricted gene flow is likely a key factor in the high genetic differentiation among C. agrestis populations. The distribution area of C. agrestis is remarkable for its extraordinarily topographic diversity with some of the world’s most rugged mountain ranges and altitudinal gradients spanning over 5,000 m (Favre et al., 2015). As a direct consequence of the uplift of the QTP, habitat fragmentation induced by complex topography and frequented climatic fluctuation was invoked as an important mechanism for the high diversity of alpine plants in this area (Wen et al., 2014; Sun, H et al., 2017). Hence, dramatically topographic and climatic changes triggered by mountain uplift probably have influenced genetic connectivity and shaped current genetic structure of C. agrestis.

The high IBR pattern of western clade, reflecting the habitat isolation and perhaps caused by complex topography, may be the cause for limited gene flow as well as observed high differentiation. The Himalayan orogeny likely triggered the vicariance among different C. agrestis groups. The north-south mountain chains and valleys may provide corridors for flora exchange between the north and south, but represent barriers to migration between the east and west (Chen et al., 2017); this likely resulted in the observed meridional differentiation of C. agrestis in the western clade. Previous studies (e.g., Gao et al., 2007; Li et al., 2011) have also suggested phylogeographic isolation associated with mountains and valleys with north-south direction. Notably, the populations located in the Hengduan Mountains (HM), exhibit high levels of genetic diversity (Table 1). As the QTP blocks the cold and dry air toward HM (Liu et al., 2013), the HM region is characterized by a much warmer and wetter climate, providing a comparatively stable environment for species. Although the western individuals are likely to possess higher genetic diversity, they are also likely to be more vulnerable to shifting disturbance regimes and environmental changes due to lack of genetic connectivity resulted from habitats isolation.

Compared to the western clade, north-south mountain barriers among different latitudes may be the reason for the divergence among the two groups in the eastern clade. A conductance grid derived from the IBR model showed low resistance to dispersal, thus high connectivity, among populations in the eastern clade. In addition, E1 and E2 exhibit a relatively low level of genetic differentiation. This phenomenon is likely due to the similar local environments among different populations resulting from the quite moderate topographical and climatic characteristics in this region (Chen et al., 2017). We detected a significantly high FIS values combined with low level of observed heterozygosity in the eastern clade (Table 1), indicating high levels of inbreeding, which may attribute to disturbance caused by human activities or the possible bottleneck events (Rands et al., 2010). There is consensus that breeding between related individuals can lead to a decrease in heterozygosity and fixation of deleterious alleles that may reduce reproductive fitness and increase the population susceptibility to environmental pressure (Kramer & Havens, 2009; Poudel et al., 2014), making populations vulnerable to cope with rapid climate changes. Notably, the highest FIS value was detected in WLG (FIS: 0.0621), indicating a key protected population for C. agrestis populations in this region.

Impact of environmental heterogeneity in population divergence

Our molecular dating based on plastome sequences suggested an early Eocene origin of C. agrestis, which is accordant with the previous estimation (Ruiz-Sanchez et al., 2012) and our results from nuclear data (Sun et al., 2020). The diversification of C. agrestis has increased since the Pliocene (Fig. 3), which is likely related with this period of intense uplift of the HM during the late Miocene to late Pliocene (Sun et al., 2011; Favre et al., 2015). The environmental and climatic fluctuation triggered by mountain uplifts can always led to divergent selection and species adaptation associated with dramatic ecological niches changes (Ren et al., 2017). Orogenic activities not only provided numerous new habitats favoring for diversification, but also promoted population divergence due to the hindered gene flow across a continuous altitudinal difference. Our biogeographic analysis revealed that the demographic evolution of C. agrestis has not simply been influenced by the orogeny at a particular time in history, but is more complex, involving repeated episodes of dispersal and subsequent vicariance events over multiple timescales. Given the EBSP analysis showing a relatively stable population size in demographic history of C. agrestis, geographical events induced environmental and climatic fluctuation that promotes population divergence, while likely had a small effect on demographic changes. Coupled with the evidence of significant IBD and IBR patterns, we speculate natural selection driven by extraordinary environmental heterogeneity and geomorphological dynamics may have profound impact in the diversification of C. agrestis. In addition, during our field work we found population sizes of C. agrestis in the Himalaya-Hengduan Mountains to be much smaller than other sites. Previous studies have indicated a significant negative effect of small population size on adaptive evolution (Nevado et al., 2019). Hence, we speculate that natural selection may play double-edged roles in the evolutionary history of any given species. On one hand, heterogeneous environments could provide more new ecological niches conducive for speciation, on the other hand, it may put the populations with weak adaptive ability to new environment in danger of extinction.

Reproductive isolation induced by geographical distance is likely to be another important factor driving the population divergence of C. agrestis. The significant population structure from AMOVA analysis, along with high level of FST values, and small population numbers discovered in the field commonly reflect there is a potential for reproductive isolation among different C. agrestis populations, especially in the western clade. For populations with small size, deleterious mutations and compensatory adaptive mutations can be quickly and independently fixed, causing reproductive isolation coupled with high level of genetic differentiation (Nielsen, 2005). However, C. agrestis exhibits low morphological diversity and no sign of expanding population demographic history. We speculate that strong environmental selective pressures are constraining morphological diversity while mutations are leading to observable population differentiation. Nonetheless, cryptic speciation cannot be ruled out, which needs further crossing experiments or/and testing for ecological niche divergence/overlap. Furthermore, significant IBR pattern suggest that genetic structure would be better explained by habitat isolation between populations than just by geographical distance alone. Therefore, habitat changes may have large effect on genetic structure for C. agrestis, indicating its sensitivity to environmental niche.

Studying local adaptation contributes to understanding the ability of populations to sustain or adapt to rapid climate change (Jia et al., 2019). We associated environmental variables with genetic structure to demonstrate the impact of environmental heterogeneity on population divergence. GF and RDA analyses indicated that covariation between genetic structure and environmental variables have played a role in promoting the diversification of C. agrestis. Specifically, both analyses found a significant association between genetic variation and temperature variables (Fig. 4; bio03, AET, bio04, bio07), suggesting temperature is potentially an important driver of genetic variation within C. agrestis, as detected in previous studies of angiosperm trees (Ahrens et al., 2019; Jia et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2019). In fact, there is growing evidence that high-mountain environments experience more rapid changes in temperature than environments at lower elevations (Pepin et al., 2015; Palazzi, Mortarini, Terzago, & von Hardenberg, 2019). Despite that, little attention has been given to understand the mechanisms of adaptive response to climate changes in high altitude areas. C. agrestis is habitually confined to grow under trees or shrubs, indicating it is sensitive to temperature. Moreover, the vertical distribution of C. agrestis is extensively wide ranging from 2,100 to 5,000 meters above sea level (Fu & Bartholomew, 2001), exhibiting a considerable differentiation in temperature. Therefore, it is reasonable that temperature variables play a vital role in promoting genetic divergence within C. agrestis. Furthermore, given the ongoing climate warming, temperature is likely still to be a key driver for adaptation of C. agrestis in future.

Genomic signatures associated with local adaptation

Simulation studies showed that the GEA method, which incorporates environmental information in the form of “environmental differentiation”, has a much lower false positive rate than genome-scan methods, and is robust to IBD pattern as well as a hierarchically structured scenario (de Villemereuil & Gaggiotti, 2015). In the present study, we first employed PCA to calculate the overall environmental differentiation and provide the first axis (PC1) to BAYESCENV as a description of the environmental distance between populations. Considering that all environmental pressures drive species divergence together, this approach could provide a realistically and comprehensively environmental gradient for studying local adaptation.

In our analysis, BAYESCENV identified a set of genes that may be involved in a continuously adaptive process. Some genes as pivotal kinases involved in signaling pathway regulation, may induce abiotic stress responses for defending harsh climate in QTP. For example, genes associated with abscisic acid (ABA) signaling pathway (SRK2EA) can regulate numerous ABA responses, such as stomata closure, to response to high altitudes with extreme difference in temperature (Sierla et al., 2018). Considering the tiny leaves of C. agrestis and their sensitivity to temperature, we speculate that stomatal regulation would be a vital process for maintaining hydration and regulating temperature. As altitude increases, hypobaric hypoxia becomes the main factor interfering with life activity. Genes related to oxidation-reduction processes involved in cellular respiration, such as NDUFS7 and NDUFB3, are of importance in facilitating adaptation of C. agrestis to high altitude areas of hypoxia. In addition, some putatively adaptive genes (CYP71 and CSI1) are associated with physiological trait regulation of vegetative and reproductive organs (Gu et al., 2010). Thus, morphological adaptations may be an essential alternative for C. agrestis to respond to environmental stress.

Despite limited available gene information in this study, it should be noted that genetic patterns of adaptation to environmental heterogeneity are likely driven by a wide range of alleles of small-effect for complex traits (Savolainen et al., 2013). The physiological processes involved in the adaptation of C. agrestis to alpine environments are most likely complex traits determined by multiple genes with potentially adaptive mechanisms including regulation of expression and epigenetic alteration (Gomulkiewicz et al., 2010; Ahrens et al., 2019). The genes identified as putatively adaptive signals in C. agrestis are most likely a part of the broader process of adaptation to climate and should constitute the first step in studying genetic mechanism of local adaptation in alpine environments. Further work with characterizing whole genome could further illuminate patterns of functional genes controlling the response to climate changes. Generally, these patterns can serve as a proxy for understanding and monitoring the adaptive process in rapidly changing environment (Ahrens et al., 2019) and inform future management strategies.

Conclusions

In this study, we demonstrate two major advances. First, we provide new insights into the genetic structure and evolutionary history of a relictual alpine herb surviving in heterogeneous environment, which can inform further conservation. Our results shed light on vital roles of mountain uplift in promoting population diversification and the dual effect of environmental heterogeneity on species evolutionary history. Second, our analyses provide a roadmap to study evolution of local adaptation of non-model species for which common gardens and genomic resources are limited. By conducting multiple analyses, we associated environmental variables with genetic variation and explicitly measured the relative influence of bioclimate on population divergence. In turn, the important environmental predictors were implemented in identifying potential loci that may under divergent selection to gain in-depth knowledge of genetic basis involved in local adaptation. More importantly, our results improve our understanding of drivers and mechanisms of adaptive evolution, aiding efforts on developing sound conservation programs in facing of rapid changing climate.
